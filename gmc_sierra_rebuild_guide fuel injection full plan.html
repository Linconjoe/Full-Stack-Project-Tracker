Project Plan: 1990 GMC Sierra 350 EFI Engine Rebuild to 300-325 HP

This report presents a comprehensive project plan for rebuilding a 1990 GMC Sierra's 350 cubic inch (5.7L) engine, now upgraded to **Electronic Fuel Injection (EFI) via a Throttle Body Injection (TBI) system**, with the objective of reliably achieving 300-325 horsepower. The plan meticulously details strategic component upgrades, emphasizes precise assembly techniques, and outlines critical tuning procedures. A core principle of this project is the exclusive use of new or factory-refurbished components, ensuring enhanced performance while maintaining the integrity and longevity of the vehicle, especially for **reliable long-distance trips (6-7 hours)**.

Achieving a 300-325 horsepower output from a stock 350 engine, which typically produces between 175 and 200 horsepower, necessitates a synergistic approach. This involves significant improvements in airflow through high-flow cylinder heads, an optimized intake manifold, and a free-flowing exhaust system. Furthermore, a carefully selected performance camshaft will optimize valve timing, fuel delivery will be enhanced via the TBI unit and precise pressure regulation, and a robust ignition system will ensure complete combustion. Crucially, supporting systems like the **cooling system, oil pump, and alternator** will be upgraded to ensure exceptional reliability and driveability during extended operation. The expected outcomes include a substantial increase in horsepower and torque, improved throttle response, and an overall enhanced driving experience, making the vehicle significantly more capable for various street and touring applications.

The project is systematically segmented into distinct, interdependent phases: Engine Disassembly & Machining, Selection and Installation of Core Engine Components, Upgrades to Fuel, Air, and Ignition Systems, Reinforcement of Supporting Systems & Driveline, and meticulous Final Tuning. Financial considerations are addressed by detailing component costs; budget estimates for a 350 Chevy build can range from over $3,000 for performance parts alone, excluding essential machine work. Machine work itself represents a significant investment, as detailed within Phase 1. This report provides detailed cost estimates for individual components where available, facilitating accurate project budgeting and planning.

Introduction: The 300-325 HP EFI 350 Vision for Reliability

Understanding the 1990 GMC Sierra's 350 Engine and EFI Upgrade

The 1990 GMC Sierra's 350 cubic inch (5.7L) engine, originally equipped with Throttle Body Injection (TBI), was a ubiquitous powerplant. While reliable, its stock output was modest. This plan upgrades it to a modern **Throttle Body Injection (TBI) EFI system** to achieve 300-325 HP. The 350 engine provides a solid and adaptable cast-iron foundation for performance upgrades due to its durable construction and widespread availability. Its enduring popularity among enthusiasts stems from its rebuildability and potential for significant power increases.

Feasibility and Advantages of Achieving 300-325 HP with TBI EFI for Long Trips

Achieving 300-325 horsepower from a 350 Chevy engine is readily attainable. By converting to a modern TBI EFI system, we gain superior fuel atomization, more precise fuel control, and self-learning capabilities compared to the original TBI or a carburetor. This leads to better cold starts, improved drivability, enhanced fuel economy, and higher ultimate power potential. For long trips, the consistent and adaptive fueling of EFI systems, combined with robust cooling, lubrication, and electrical systems, ensures reliable and stress-free operation. Strategic selection of performance components, coupled with proper ECM tuning, will effectively mitigate challenges and allow the TBI EFI platform to support the 300-325 horsepower target reliably for extended periods.

Phase 1: Engine Disassembly, Inspection, and Machining

Initial Assessment and Engine Removal

The project commences with a thorough initial assessment of the existing engine's condition. This preliminary step is crucial for identifying any pre-existing issues that might affect the rebuild, such as persistent oil leaks, signs of chronic overheating, or a noticeable loss of power, which are common problems observed in aging Chevy 350 engines. A comprehensive evaluation at this stage helps determine the extent of wear and potential damage, guiding subsequent decisions regarding component replacement or reconditioning. Following this assessment, the engine must be safely removed from the vehicle. This operation requires appropriate lifting equipment, such as a robust engine hoist or lift, to ensure the safety of the technician and prevent any damage to the engine or vehicle during the extraction process. Proper preparation, including disconnecting all electrical, fuel, and cooling lines, is essential before lifting the engine.

Detailed Component Inspection and Wear Analysis

Upon removal, the engine is completely disassembled, and each individual component undergoes meticulous inspection for signs of wear, cracks, or damage. This critical evaluation includes assessing the reusability of core components such as the cylinder block, crankshaft, and connecting rods, which are foundational to the engine's integrity. Cylinder bores are checked for taper and out-of-round conditions, crankshaft journals are inspected for scoring or excessive wear, and connecting rods are verified for straightness and twist. Prior to any further work or measurement, all parts must be thoroughly cleaned. This involves using a soap and water solution, a solvent tank, or brake cleaner to remove accumulated debris, grime, and oil from operation, as well as any residue from previous machining processes. This cleaning is vital as it allows for accurate inspection and measurement, ensuring that no hidden defects compromise the rebuild.

Rotating Assembly (Details)

Goal:

Reliable foundation. Stock crank/rods often reusable after machining for 300-325HP. Basic piston/ring package sufficient, but quality is key for reliability.

  • **Roller Rocker Arms:**
    • Steel Roller Tip Rocker Arms (e.g., 1.6 Ratio, 3/8" Stud for SBC 350). Available new. (Est. cost: $150 - $250)
    • Aluminum Roller Rocker Arms (e.g., Vortec 305 350 SBC Chevy Aluminum Roller Rockers 1.6 Ratio 3/8" Self Aligning). Available new. (Est. cost: $200 - $300)
    • Summit Racing Pro Series Stainless Rocker Arms (PN: SUM-G6990, 1.5 Ratio, 3/8" Stud, Full Roller). Available new.
    • Scorpion Racing Products (PN: SCP1036, 1.65 Ratio, Vortec Roller Rockers). Available new (or as "new other" with minor cosmetic imperfections).

Compatibility is KEY. Mismatched components (e.g., cam lift vs. spring capability, pushrod strength) lead to failure or poor performance. Quality components enhance reliability for extended use.

Oil Pump & Lubrication System (Details)

Goal:

Maintain consistent and adequate oil pressure to all engine components, especially under sustained high-load conditions during long trips.

Components (New/Refurbished):

  • **High-Volume Oil Pump:**
    • Melling M-55HV or M-155HV (for small block Chevy) - Provides increased oil flow, beneficial for performance engines and those operating for extended periods. Available new. (Est. cost: $50 - $100)
    • Ensure proper oil pump drive shaft (e.g., ARP high-strength) is used with a high-volume pump. Available new. (Est. cost: $20 - $40)
  • **Oil Pan:** Ensure your oil pan has adequate capacity and baffling for consistent oil pickup during acceleration/braking. Stock pan is often sufficient for street use, but consider aftermarket if sustained high-RPM or cornering is anticipated.

Proper lubrication is paramount for engine longevity, particularly when increasing horsepower and planning for long-distance reliability. Do not compromise on oil pump quality.

Intake Manifold (EFI Compatible) (Details)

Goal:

Provide a suitable mounting surface for the 4-barrel TBI unit and optimize air distribution.

Options (New/Refurbished):

  • **Edelbrock Performer RPM Air-Gap Intake Manifold (PN: 7501 or 7516 for Vortec heads):** Excellent choice for street performance, providing good low-end torque and mid-range power. Its dual-plane, air-gap design helps keep the intake charge cool. Available new. (Est. cost: $300 - $450)
  • **Weiand Stealth Intake Manifold:** Similar performance characteristics to the Edelbrock Performer RPM, often a good alternative.
  • **Any 4150-flange 4-barrel intake manifold:** Most aftermarket performance intake manifolds designed for carburetors will accept a TBI unit. Ensure it matches your cylinder head port design (e.g., standard or Vortec).

The TBI unit replaces the carburetor on a standard 4-barrel intake. A dual-plane design is generally preferred for street drivability and torque.

Fuel Delivery System (High Pressure) (Details)

Goal:

Supply consistent high-pressure fuel to the TBI unit. Crucial for EFI reliability.

Components (New):

  • **High-Pressure Electric Fuel Pump:**
    • In-tank or external electric fuel pump capable of 40-60+ psi (e.g., Walbro 255 LPH, Aeromotive, Holley). Many EFI kits offer specific pumps. Available new. (Est. cost: $100 - $200)
  • **EFI-Specific Fuel Pressure Regulator:**
    • Required to maintain consistent fuel pressure. Some TBI units have integrated regulators; others require an external adjustable unit (e.g., Aeromotive, Holley). Available new. (Est. cost: $80 - $180)
  • **Return Fuel Line:** Essential for proper EFI operation to prevent fuel heating and maintain stable pressure. Requires a return line back to the fuel tank.
  • **Upgraded Fuel Lines:**
    • New fuel lines (e.g., 3/8" or larger feed, 5/16" or 3/8" return) capable of handling higher pressure. PTFE-lined braided stainless steel or high-pressure rubber hose with AN fittings are recommended for durability and safety on long trips. Available new. (Est. cost: $150 - $400 for lines and fittings)
  • **Fuel Filters:** Pre-filter (100 micron) before the pump and post-filter (10 micron) after the pump for injector protection. Available new. (Est. cost: $30 - $60)

A robust and correctly sized high-pressure fuel system is non-negotiable for reliable EFI operation, especially for extended periods. Do not reuse old carbureted fuel lines or pumps.

Ignition System (EFI Compatible) (Details)

Goal:

Strong, consistent spark, controlled by the EFI ECM for complete combustion and optimal timing.

Components (New/Refurbished):

  • **Distributor:**
    • EFI-compatible distributor (e.g., a "ready-to-run" HEI with mechanical/vacuum advance locked out, or a magnetic pickup/Hall effect distributor). Many EFI systems (like ACES and Holley) recommend their own compatible distributors for plug-and-play timing control. Available new. (Est. cost: $200 - $350)
  • **Ignition Coil:**
    • High-output coil (e.g., ACCEL, MSD) - compatible with your chosen EFI system. Available new. (Est. cost: $50 - $100)
  • **Spark Plug Wires:**
    • Low-resistance, high-temperature wires (e.g., 8mm or 8.5mm performance wires). Available new. (Est. cost: $50 - $100)
  • **Spark Plugs:** Performance spark plugs matched to the engine's compression and ignition requirements. Available new. (Est. cost: $30 - $50 for 8)

The EFI ECM will precisely control ignition timing, which is crucial for maximizing power and efficiency, especially for a 300-325 HP target and long-trip reliability.

Cooling System (Details)

Goal:

Manage increased heat effectively to prevent overheating and ensure engine longevity, especially during 6-7 hour trips.

Components (New/Refurbished):

  • **Radiator:** High-flow, larger capacity aluminum radiator (e.g., 2-row or 3-row core). This is critical for efficient heat dissipation. Available new. (Est. cost: $200 - $400)
  • **Electric Cooling Fans:** Dual electric fans with a proper shroud for maximum airflow, controlled by the EFI system or a dedicated thermostat controller. These provide consistent cooling regardless of vehicle speed. Available new. (Est. cost: $150 - $300)
  • **High-Flow Water Pump:** Ensures adequate coolant circulation. Available new. (Est. cost: $50 - $100)
  • **Low-Temperature Thermostat:** (e.g., 160°F or 180°F) to allow the engine to run cooler, reducing thermal stress. Available new. (Est. cost: $15 - $30)
  • **Quality Coolant Hoses and Clamps:** Replace old, fatigued hoses to prevent leaks and blowouts.

Overheating is a primary cause of engine failure, particularly in performance applications and during sustained highway driving. Invest in a robust cooling system for peace of mind.

Alternator Upgrade (Details)

Goal:

Provide sufficient electrical power for the new EFI system, electric cooling fans, and other accessories, preventing voltage drops and ensuring reliable operation.

Components (New/Refurbished):

  • **High-Output Alternator:**
    • Upgrade from stock (typically 60-80 amp) to a 100-140+ amp alternator. Modern EFI systems and powerful electric fans draw significant current. Brands like Powermaster, ACDelco (high-output reman), or aftermarket high-amp units. Available new or quality refurbished. (Est. cost: $150 - $300)
  • **Heavy-Duty Wiring:** Consider upgrading the main power wire from the alternator to the battery/fuse box to a larger gauge (e.g., 4-gauge or 2-gauge) to handle the increased current safely.

An under-sized alternator can lead to low voltage, affecting EFI performance, charging issues, and potential damage to electrical components. Essential for long-trip reliability.

Driveline Upgrades (Details)

Goal:

Reliably transfer 300-325 HP to the wheels and prevent component failure under increased stress.

Components (New/Refurbished):

The driveline is the next critical system after the engine. Reinforcing it ensures the power reaches the ground reliably and safely, especially important for extended driving and preventing breakdowns.

EFI System Fundamentals (TBI) (Details)

Modern TBI EFI systems offer superior fuel atomization, precise fuel control, and self-learning capabilities. They adapt to changes in altitude, temperature, and engine load, leading to better cold starts, improved drivability, enhanced fuel economy, and higher ultimate power potential compared to carburetors. For long trips, this adaptability is crucial for consistent performance and efficiency.

Benefits:

Excellent drivability, improved cold starts, better fuel economy, adaptability to conditions, and good power potential.

Drawbacks:

Initial setup can be complex for first-timers, requires a high-pressure fuel system, and optimal performance often benefits from professional tuning.

ECM Tuning Options (EFI) (Details)

Options:

  • **Self-Learning (Initial Setup):** Most TBI EFI systems feature a calibration wizard for initial setup and then "self-learn" as you drive. This gets the engine running well for most street applications.
  • **Handheld Tuner Adjustments:** The included handheld display allows for monitoring various parameters and making basic adjustments to fuel, timing, and idle settings.
  • **Laptop Software Tuning:** For maximizing the 300-325 HP target, optimizing drivability, and handling specific engine characteristics (e.g., aggressive camshafts, power adders), laptop-based tuning software (provided by the EFI manufacturer) is highly recommended. This allows for fine-tuning fuel maps, ignition tables, idle control, and other advanced parameters.
  • **Professional Dyno Tuning:** For the absolute best performance, efficiency, and reliability, especially for long trips, a professional dyno tune is invaluable. A skilled tuner can optimize the EFI system for your specific engine combination and driving conditions, ensuring maximum safe power and optimal drivability. (Est. labor cost: $500 - $1000+)

A proper EFI ECM and tuning strategy are vital for safety, efficiency, reliability, and achieving 300-325 HP with optimal drivability across all conditions, including extended highway cruising.

Monitoring: Wideband O2 Sensor (Details)

Indispensable for accurate tuning and real-time monitoring of your engine's health. A wideband O2 sensor provides precise Air-Fuel Ratio (AFR) data across a broad range, which is critical for ensuring the engine is not running too rich (wasting fuel, fouling plugs) or too lean (causing engine damage). Many EFI systems include one, but if not, a standalone unit is a must-have.

Realizing Your 300-325 HP Goal with EFI for Reliability

Achieving 300-325 horsepower from a 1990 GMC Sierra's 350 engine with a modern EFI (TBI) conversion is an ambitious and highly rewarding goal. This guide outlines a strategic combination of key upgrades, including high-flow cylinder heads, a performance-oriented camshaft, optimized intake and exhaust systems, and a robust TBI EFI fuel and ignition setup.
